# Median Per Captital Income >> Gini Coefficient | India

There is a recurring temptation in Indian policy circles to reach for a single number when a complex question is asked. The Gini coefficient is one of those numbers. It is neat, it is comparable across countries, and it fits on a slide. But like most single numbers that describe complex systems, it tells you something, but not everything, and in India's case, the "not everything" part is doing a lot of work.

## What the Gini Actually Is

The Gini coefficient measures how far the actual distribution of income or consumption deviates from perfect equality. It runs from 0 (everyone earns the same) to 100 (one person earns everything). The [World Bank computes it from household surveys](https://data.worldbank.org/indicator/SI.POV.GINI), and for most purposes in countries like India, it is derived from consumption rather than income, since income in a predominantly informal economy is simply not measurable at the household level.

The coefficient satisfies some desirable theoretical properties. It is scale-invariant, population-invariant, and sensitive to transfers across the distribution. In bounded uses -- tracking the same country over consistent methodology across time, or anchoring rough cross-country comparisons -- it gives you a directional signal worth examining. But these properties only get you so far.

## Where It Genuinely Fails India

India's Gini is computed from consumption surveys, not income data. [This is not an accident](https://www.indiaspend.com/explainers/explained-why-indias-inequality-is-underestimated-962681). In an economy with over 80% informal employment, income is simply not measurable in a household survey for most people. So analysts use consumption as a proxy. The problem is that consumption compresses the distribution at both ends. High-income earners save a large fraction of their income, so their consumption understates their income substantially. The result is a consumption Gini that reads as 25.5, while the [income-based Gini from the World Inequality Database sits at 0.61](https://www.insightsonindia.com/2025/07/14/state-of-inequality-in-india/), among the highest in the world. These are not measuring the same thing.

Compounding this, [household surveys structurally miss the very top of the distribution](https://idronline.org/article/inequality/inequality-metrics-dont-capture-indias-reality/). The richest households either refuse to participate or significantly under-report. If 90% of the population looks relatively equal and the real action is in the top 1%, missing that group alone can make a deeply unequal country look egalitarian on paper. The Gini is not just an imprecise instrument here. It is pointing in the wrong direction.

There is also a deeper structural critique, which comes from [research published in Nature Human Behaviour](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41562-022-01430-7). The Gini is a single-parameter measure that cannot distinguish between inequality concentrated at the bottom versus the top of the distribution. Two countries with a Gini of 0.40 can have radically different social realities. A single number compressing an entire distribution loses exactly the information that matters most for policy.

## The Pritchett Argument: The Median Is the Message

This is where Lant Pritchett's work becomes essential, and it shifts the framing entirely.

Pritchett ran the numbers across 389 countries and time periods, asking one question: what actually explains why some countries have more poverty than others? The answer turned out to be almost embarrassingly simple. One variable -- median consumption, what the person in the middle of the income ladder spends -- explains virtually everything. The statistical fit is near-perfect, as close to a law of nature as economics ever gets.

Once you know the median, everything else -- the shape of the distribution, how unequal the country is, whether the rich are very rich or just moderately rich -- adds almost nothing to your understanding of poverty. At most 1.2% of the variation is left to explain. And packed into that 1.2% is every government anti-poverty scheme, every cash transfer program, every carefully designed intervention that development economists spend their careers debating.

Put plainly: the reason India has the poverty rate it does is that its median income is where it is. Not because of how income is distributed around that median. Not because of what the Gini says.

## Why Median Per Capita Income Is a Better Frame for India

Median per capita consumption (or income, where measurable) has several structural advantages over the Gini as a primary indicator for a country at India's current stage.

First, it is honest about what the actual problem is. India is not primarily suffering from maldistribution. It is suffering from a low base. The Kuznets framework predicted decades ago that inequality typically rises during early industrialization as a structural feature of the transition from agriculture to manufacturing, not as a policy failure. At India's current income level, the gap between the median Indian and the median Dane is the overwhelming fact. The Gini obscures this by being scale-invariant; it tells you about shape while ignoring the level entirely.

Second, the median is harder to game. A government can move the consumption Gini through survey methodology choices, definitional changes, or by simply not surveying urban slums adequately. The median consumption level, while not immune to data problems, is more anchored to the material reality of the household at the centre of the distribution. It is a number with a physical referent.

Third, and most importantly, the median is a direct measure of what Pritchett calls ["inclusive-enough growth"](https://lantpritchett.org/the-case-for-sustained-rapid-inclusive-enough-economic-growth-as-a-focus-of-development/): sustained, broad-based increases in living standards that reach the middle of the distribution. Growth is inclusive enough when it raises the median, even if the Gini stays flat or rises somewhat. Vietnam and Bangladesh both grew their median consumption rapidly while their Gini movements were modest and mixed. Their poverty rates collapsed. The median tracked the story. The Gini did not.

Pritchett's further finding is that [growth in median consumption is not just sufficient for poverty reduction -- it is empirically necessary](https://lantpritchett.org/the-case-for-sustained-rapid-inclusive-enough-economic-growth-as-a-focus-of-development/). There are no countries in the data that achieved low poverty at low median incomes. None. In contrast, countries with high medians have uniformly low poverty regardless of their Gini. This asymmetry tells you everything about which variable is carrying the load.

## What This Means for How India Should Think About Itself

India's median consumption remains a small fraction of what citizens in rich democracies take for granted. [Pritchett has pointed out](https://www.mercatus.org/ideasofindia/lant-pritchett-reforming-development-economics) that Bihar sits at roughly Haiti's per capita GDP while Goa sits at Jordan's -- within a single country. The question that should dominate the development conversation is: how fast is the median rising, and is it rising across states?

The Gini is not useless. Tracked consistently over time on the same survey methodology, it offers a signal. Used alongside the Palma ratio or decile shares, it adds texture. But as the primary lens through which India evaluates its development progress, it is the wrong instrument. It measures shape when what India urgently needs to track is elevation.

The median per capita consumption or income -- rising, consistently, across regions and demographic groups -- is the number that matters. Everything else is commentary.
